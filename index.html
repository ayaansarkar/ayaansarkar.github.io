Historical Overview

In 1351, Ibn Battuta left Fez to begin his journey to Mali, crossing the Sahara Desert. At this time, Mali was one of the richest empires. Mali was known for its vast gold resources and flourishing cities. During Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca, he donated so much gold that there was a depression in Egypt. Mali was largely known for being a grand trading city, often engaging in the trans-Saharan gold-salt trade. Ibn Battuta spent 8 months in Mali, where he documented his experiences.

Spread of Islam

By the time Ibn Battuta arrived in Mali, Islam had already become a widespread religion. The rulers, officials, and merchants in the city all followed Islam. Everyone was to abide by the Islamic Law or Sharia. The rulers established Mosques and encouraged praying and Islamic Education. Ibn Battuta observed the application of Islam in courts and in trade. The trade across the Sahara was maintained by Islamic merchants and caravans.

Local Culture

Even though Mali was an Islamic kingdom, Ibn Battuta noted many customs that were different from what he considered to be proper Islamic behavior. He was surprised by the freedom and respect that women had. He noted that they did not wear veils. He also said that there were many live musical festivals. There were also some customs, such as where rams were used as protection from the evil eye, that showed how the local culture mixed with Islamic culture. Ibn Battuta sometimes disagreed with the social practices of Mali.

Trading Customs

Trade was the main source of Mali's wealth and power. The empire prospered on the exchange of gold and salt, which were equally as valuable at the time. Salt from Taghaza was traded for gold dust from Mali. These were traded along the Sahara Desert by merchants traveling in caravans. Mali's lesser known traded items include ivory, kola nuts, ostrich feathers, hides, and slaves. Ibn Battuta talked about the busy markets that took over the city. He described how merchants had an important role in organizing and protecting the trade routes.
